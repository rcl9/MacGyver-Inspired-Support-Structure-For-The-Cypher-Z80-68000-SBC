# A new 'MacGyver Inspired' Support Structure for the Cypher Z80-68000 Single Board Computer

This repository documents the thought process and build process of a new supporting structure to hold all of the components of the 1984-era [Motel Computers Cypher Z80/68000 SBC](https://github.com/rcl9/Cypher-Z80-68000-Single-Board-Computer-1984-by-Motel-Computers---History-and-Documentation). I've built a lot of things during my lifetime but this is the first project whereby I'd consider it to have a 'MacGyver Inspired' outcome. 

For 40+ years I have had all of the components sitting on a square piece of pegboard but have clearly wanted to make a more portable and structurally sound support structure for it. However, I could never figure out how to turn my mental image of the ideal setup into reality, until now.

## My Goals and Intentions

The fundamental problems with the original arrangement were as follows:

- It was very cumbersome to carry from my storage location to a workbench. The 22MB hard disk, 5-1/4" floppy disk and two XT-style power supplies were heavy and could easily fall off.

- It was not portable nor transportable whatsoever.

- The two XT-style power supplies from the 1984 era were getting long in the tooth. I didn't want them to fail and take down the impossible to replace components making up this system. 

My goals for this project were:

- Something "more vertical" and portable, such as two pieces of vertical plywood onto which would be attached the components with a carrying handle at the top.

- One shelf should hold the SBC + "port organizer add-on", and the other shelf should hold the 22MB hard disk + SASI controller card, 5-1/4" floppy disk, a new switched mode power supply and AC adapter socket. Very careful attention would have to be made to ensure that these devices could still hook up to the main SBC via their existing short ribbon cables + 5/12v power cables.

- Since the 22MB hard disk + SASI controller card and 5-1/4" floppy disk would not be used too often, two power switches would be used to cut the 5/12v power rails to those devices.

## The Visual Build Process

I was stuck with "writer's block" for most of 40 years and could just not figure out how to build this new supporting structure that I had long envisioned in my mind. However, this time around I needed to revamp and update the hardware + PSUs for this SBC so it was the right (and only) time to also create the new supporting setup. 

In my mind I decided that I needed a 2-level wire mesh stand, of a very specific set of dimensions + inter-shelf spacing, with the lower 6" open for my dual 8" Calcomp floppy disk drives. This time around I opted to go to Google's Gemini AI and provide it with my ideal setup. One thing lead to another and I eventually stumbled across the perfect solution, aka an ideal 'MacGyver solution' which was a dual adjustable mesh shelf from Amazon Basics:

<div style="text-align:center">
<img src="/Images/amazon_mesh_shelf.jpg" alt="" style="width:70%; height:auto;">
</div>
<br>

This design allowed the shelves to be adjusted on the 4 legs using unique "compression fit" plastic collar sleeves. That would turn out to be both a blessing and a curse: it was ideal for adjusting the shelves to the exact height placements I needed; however, picking it up by each shelf would dislodge that shelf from its compression fitting since it was not designed to be lifted in such a manner.

The first effort was to set up the shelf spacings for the ideal placement I'd eventually need, including the 6" lower space for my dual 8" Calcomp floppy disk drives. I used plyers to squeeze the compression fittings as much as I could. With that set up, I used a hack saw to cut the excess pipe off from the 4 legs (and add a rubber chair stop to the top of each cut pipe):

<div style="text-align:center">
<img src="/Images/img03.webp" alt="Cut pipes" style="width:70%; height:auto;">
</div>
<br>

I was not sure how I'd overcome the problem of the compression fittings coming loose when you picked up a shelf with weight on it, other than to warn the user "not to do that". After a couple of days I stumbled across some parts in my bins that gave me the (final) ideal solution: use "mirror holding clips" above and below each of the compression fittings (12 in total).

Prior to coming up with the idea of the mirror clips I had decided to build a "support + carrying tray" for the shelving unit. It had two purposes: (1) to provide support so that a person did not have to pick it up via the mesh shelves (which would otherwise cause the compression fittings to come loose) and (2) to provide a means to add two carrying handles to the outside. However, I had no idea at all about how I was going to build it to the exacting specifications I needed and wanted.
